## 1. Organizer liability / negligence (free community walking events)

**Finding 1.1 — Standard negligence framework applies.** An event organizer in Canada owes participants a common-law duty of care to take reasonable steps against *foreseeable* harm. Liability requires duty + breach of the standard of a reasonably prudent organizer + causation + damage. Canadian commentary on event liability (mostly festival/concert-focused, but the principles scale down) consistently frames organizer exposure around planning failures: unsafe route/site choices, ignoring known hazards, no emergency plan.
→ Sources: [HSP Law, "Injuries at Concerts and Entertainment Events in Ontario"](https://www.hsplaw.ca/blog/ensuring-your-safety-navigating-injuries-at-concerts-and-entertainment-events-in-ontario); [TSF Law, "Festival & Outdoor Event Injuries"](https://tsflaw.ca/injury-festival-outdoor-event/); [Sharma Law, "Enjoying Festivals Safely"](https://vsharmalaw.ca/enjoying-festivals-safely-liability-and-personal-injury-risks/). No NB-specific case on volunteer walking-group organizers was located — **UNVERIFIED whether any NB court has ever considered a free walking-meetup organizer's duty** (likely none; this is a low-litigation category).

**Finding 1.2 — Realistic risk profile is low but not zero.** For 3–6 adults walking on downtown sidewalks and public squares: the main foreseeable perils are slips/trips (esp. winter ice), traffic incidents at crossings, medical events, and (at pub-ending variants) alcohol-related issues (see §4). Because participants are adults voluntarily walking in public — something they could do alone — the *incremental* duty created by organizing is modest: pick a reasonable route, don't lead people into obvious hazards, respond reasonably to incidents. The organizer is not an insurer of safety. That said, an unincorporated individual organizer is personally named in any claim (no corporate shield until incorporation — see §5).
→ Source: general negligence principles per above; [Ghabour Law, "Legal Liability at Public and Community Events"](https://ghabourlaw.com/legal-liability-at-public-and-community-events-understanding-fault-and-responsibilities/) (US-flavoured but consistent framing). Characterization of "low risk" is analytical, not sourced — **flag for lawyer confirmation**.

**Standard mitigations (sector practice from volunteer-led outdoor clubs):** route pre-walk/hazard scan, participant info + emergency contact, leader briefing on risks, incident log, first-aid basics, cancellation criteria (ice storms, extreme weather), waiver/informed-consent (see §3), CGL insurance (see §5).
→ Sources: [Alpine Club of Canada — Waivers & Insurance FAQ (2024)](https://alpineclubofcanada.ca/wp-content/uploads/2024/05/Waiver-FAQ-Insurance-01_02_2023.docx.pdf); [ACC Vancouver Island — Trip Leaders: Waivers and Liability](https://accvi.ca/trip-leaders/waivers-and-liability/); [IMBA Canada — General Liability for Clubs](https://imbacanada.com/general-liability-for-clubs/).

**Actions:** (a) Written safety protocol + route checklist — **now**; (b) incident-report template + emergency-contact collection — **before-first-pilot**; (c) CGL policy naming organizer/entity — **before-first-pilot** (see §5).

## 2. Occupiers' liability in New Brunswick

**Finding 2.1 — NB abolished occupiers' liability, but the "2011" date in the brief is wrong.** NB never had an Occupiers' Liability Act to repeal; it legislatively **abolished the common-law occupiers' liability categories** via the *Law Reform Act*, S.N.B. 1993, c. L-1.2, s. 2, **effective June 1, 1994**. The "2011" figure is the *Revised Statutes of New Brunswick 2011* consolidation citation — the Act is now cited as *Law Reform Act*, **RSNB 2011, c 184, s 2(1)**. Matters that would have been occupiers'-liability cases "shall be determined in accordance with other rules of liability" — i.e., **ordinary negligence**. Section 2 also allows damages to be *reduced* on account of the plaintiff's trespass.
→ Sources: [Law Reform Act, RSNB 2011, c 184 (CanLII)](https://www.canlii.org/en/nb/laws/stat/rsnb-2011-c-184/latest/rsnb-2011-c-184.html) (page bot-blocked; text confirmed via CanLII search extracts); [MacGillivray Law, "Slip and fall liability in New Brunswick"](https://macgillivraylaw.com/slip-and-falls/law-nb); [Sask. Law Reform Commission, *Reform of Occupiers' Liability Law* consultation report (2023)](https://lawreformcommission.sk.ca/Occupiers-Liability-Consultation-Report-Final-03-23-2023.pdf) (surveys NB's abolition-in-favour-of-negligence model); [UBC Wiki, Torts — Occupiers](https://wiki.ubc.ca/Documentation:Torts/Negligence_Occupiers). I could not open the statute page itself through this environment's proxy — **exact subsection wording should be pulled from laws.gnb.ca by the reviewing lawyer**.

**Finding 2.2 — Who bears what.** At partner cafés/pubs: the *venue operator* bears the ordinary-negligence duty to provide reasonably safe premises for patrons (wet floors, stairs, crowding). In public parks/sidewalks: the *City of Fredericton* (or the Province, for provincial property) bears premises-related negligence exposure, subject to municipal-liability doctrines (e.g., policy/operational distinctions and any statutory notice periods for claims against municipalities — **UNVERIFIED whether NB's Local Governance Act imposes a short notice period for sidewalk claims; check**). The walking-group organizer is generally *not* an occupier of public streets or of the café, so premises claims should flow to venue/City first; organizer exposure would be secondary (e.g., negligently leading the group onto a known-dangerous surface).
→ Sources: [MacGillivray Law, NB slip-and-fall page](https://macgillivraylaw.com/slip-and-falls/law-nb); [Foster & Company (Fredericton firm), "Duty of Care Limits on Occupiers' Liability Principles"](https://fosterandcompany.com/duty-of-care-limits-on-occupiers-liability-principles/) (NB vs NS contrast; page bot-blocked, relied on search extract).

**Implication:** NB's negligence-only regime is actually simpler and mildly favourable: no statutory occupier categories, and the organizer's non-occupier status is clear. **Actions:** (a) In partner-café agreements, note venue retains premises responsibility and holds its own CGL + liquor liability — **before-first-pilot**; (b) route selection avoids known-hazard surfaces; winter protocol — **before-first-pilot**.

## 3. Waiver enforceability (NB/Canada) for free recreational events

**Finding 3.1 — Elements.** Canadian courts (common-law provinces incl. NB) enforce recreational waivers where: (1) clear, unambiguous wording expressly covering **negligence** of the organizer; (2) reasonable steps to bring onerous terms to the signer's attention (headings, bold text, time to read, not rushed); (3) signed by a capable adult before the activity; (4) scope covers the activity that caused injury. Leading authority: *Karroll v Silver Star Mountain Resorts Ltd.*, 1988 CanLII 3294 (BCSC, McLachlin J) — a signature binds unless a reasonable person would know the signer didn't intend to agree AND the proponent failed to take reasonable steps to draw terms to attention. Counter-example: *Zaky v 2285771 Ontario Inc.* (2020, ONSC) — waiver failed where participant was rushed. Online/electronic waivers have been upheld (BC).
→ Sources: [Karroll (CanLII)](https://www.canlii.org/en/bc/bcsc/doc/1988/1988canlii3294/1988canlii3294.html); [Nelligan Law, "Recreational Sports and Liability Waivers"](https://nelliganlaw.ca/recreational-sports-and-liability-waivers-can-you-still-make-a-claim-in-ontario/); [Bergeron Clifford, "When Are Liability Waivers Enforceable?"](https://www.bergeronclifford.com/blog/liability-waivers-and-when-they-are-enforceable); [Norton Rose Fulbright, "Online waivers enforceable for recreational activities in BC"](https://www.nortonrosefulbright.com/en-ca/knowledge/publications/94a553cd/online-waivers-enforceable-for-recreational-activities-in-bc); [Beard Winter, "Waivers in Recreational and Sporting Activities from A to Z" (PDF)](https://www.beardwinter.com/content/uploads/Waivers-in-Recreational-and-Sporting-Activities-from-A-to-Z.pdf). **UNVERIFIED: no NB-specific reported waiver decision located** — NB courts would apply the same common-law framework (*Karroll*/*Tercon* line), but confirm with counsel.

**Finding 3.2 — Limits.** Waivers don't cover what they don't clearly say; consumer-protection carve-outs exist in some provinces (Ontario's debate post-*Schnarr*; NB position **UNVERIFIED**); no waiver binds non-participants (bystanders, third parties); gross-misconduct coverage is doubtful; Quebec disallows them (irrelevant here but relevant if replicated).
→ Sources: [Mondaq, "Liability Waivers: Are They Enforceable?"](https://www.mondaq.com/canada/Consumer-Protection/814910/Liability-Waivers-Are-They-Enforceable); [ACC Waiver FAQ](https://alpineclubofcanada.ca/wp-content/uploads/2024/05/Waiver-FAQ-Insurance-01_02_2023.docx.pdf) (notes Quebec exception; leader-witnessed signing as best practice).

**Finding 3.3 — Is a waiver even the right tool?** For a low-risk, free, adults-only walking event, sector practice supports a **lightweight "informed consent + assumption of risk" acknowledgment** (signed once at registration, electronically) rather than an aggressive full release: it preserves goodwill, documents risk disclosure (which itself defeats many claims via *volenti*/contributory negligence), and still can include a negligence release clause. Insurance, not paper, is the primary financial protection. There is also consideration doubt for free events (waiver as contract needs consideration — participation itself usually suffices, but **UNVERIFIED in NB**).
→ Sources: [Sport Law, "Waivers: Format and Management"](https://sportlaw.ca/waivers-format-and-management-during-covid-19-and-beyond/); [Siskinds, "What is the effect of signing a waiver?"](https://www.siskinds.com/effect-of-signing-a-waiver/); ACC/ACCVI practice above.

**Actions:** (a) Draft one-page plain-language informed-consent + release (covers negligence explicitly, bold heading, unhurried e-signature at registration, adults only) — **before-first-pilot**, lawyer-reviewed; (b) keep signed records + version control — **before-first-pilot**; (c) revisit robustness of waiver if activities expand beyond walking — **before-scale**.

## 4. Alcohol / social host liability (pub-ending expeditions)

**Finding 4.1 — *Childs v Desormeaux*, 2006 SCC 18.** SCC held social hosts of a BYOB party owed **no duty of care to third parties** injured by an intoxicated guest: hosts didn't serve, didn't monitor, no statutory duty, and hosting alone doesn't create risk. The Court left the door open where the host serves/controls alcohol, knows of intoxication + intent to drive, invites guests into an inherently risky environment, or has a paternalistic relationship.
→ Sources: [Childs v. Desormeaux — Wikipedia](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Childs_v._Desormeaux); [Siskinds, "A refresher on social host liability"](https://www.siskinds.com/tis-the-season-a-refresher-on-social-host-liability/); [LawNow, "Whatever Happened to Childs v. Desormeaux"](https://www.lawnow.org/whatever-happened-childs-v-desormeaux-killer-hospitality/).

**Finding 4.2 — Post-*Childs* evolution.** *Williams v Richard*, 2018 ONCA 889 and *Wardak v Froom* (2017 ONSC) show duty analysis is fact-specific: (1) foreseeability = host's knowledge of intoxication/dangerous plan; (2) proximity = "something more" (inviting guests into risky environment; paternalistic relationship). Duty to *guests themselves* is more plausible than to third parties. Commercial hosts (licensed pubs) owe a well-established duty (*Jordan House v Menow*, SCC 1973 line) to patrons and third parties — that duty sits with the **licensed venue**, not the meetup organizer.
→ Sources: [Williams v. Richard, 2018 ONCA 889 (Ontario Courts)](https://www.ontariocourts.ca/decisions/2018/2018ONCA0889.htm); [CanLII Connects summary of Williams](https://canliiconnects.org/en/summaries/66660); [CanLII Connects, Wardak v Froom](https://canliiconnects.org/en/summaries/55109); [SG Injury Law, Social Host Liability Update](https://www.sginjurylaw.ca/about/blog/personal-injury-cases/social-host-liability-update/). (*Jordan House* cite from general knowledge — **UNVERIFIED via fetched source**, standard authority.)

**Finding 4.3 — Application to Bilingua Quest.** Where the event *ends* at a licensed café/pub and participants buy their own drinks from the licensee, the organizer is closer to the *Childs* no-duty position than even a house-party host: no serving, no supply, no control, licensed commercial host present who bears the service duty. Exposure rises if the organizer supplies alcohol, hosts drinking games, pressures consumption, or knowingly lets a visibly impaired participant drive.
**Best practices:** organizer never buys rounds/serves; event formally "ends" at arrival (or at a stated time) with drinks as an optional social extension; leaders trained to offer taxi/safe-ride info if someone appears impaired; maintain alcohol-free variants; confirm venue's liquor licence + liquor liability insurance; keep 18+ but note **NB drinking age is 19** — an 18-year-old participant cannot legally be served (consider 19+ for pub variants). NB liquor-licensing point from general knowledge — **UNVERIFIED via fetched source; confirm NB drinking age 19 with counsel/NB Liquor**.

**Actions:** (a) Written alcohol policy (no organizer-supplied alcohol; end-of-event definition; impairment response) — **before-first-pilot**; (b) 19+ rule for pub-ending variants — **before-first-pilot**; (c) liquor-liability extension on CGL only if the org ever hosts its own licensed events — **before-scale**.

## 5. Volunteer host liability + insurance

**Finding 5.1 — NB has NO general volunteer-protection statute.** Nova Scotia's *Volunteer Protection Act*, SNS 2002, c 14 is described as **the only stand-alone Canadian statute** shielding nonprofit volunteers from personal liability. New Brunswick's closest analogue is the narrow *Volunteer Emergency Aid Act*, RSNB 2016, c 17 (Good-Samaritan protection for voluntary emergency aid at accident/medical-emergency scenes only). So NB volunteer hosts have no statutory immunity; their protection comes from (a) the organization's incorporation, (b) insurance, (c) ordinary negligence defences.
→ Sources: [Volunteer Protection Act, SNS 2002, c 14 (CanLII)](https://www.canlii.org/en/ns/laws/stat/sns-2002-c-14/latest/sns-2002-c-14.html); [Cox & Palmer, "The Volunteer Protection Act"](https://coxandpalmerlaw.com/publication/the-volunteer-protection-act-how-to-protect-your-volunteers/); [Gray Whitley, "Nova Scotia's Unique Volunteer Protection Legislation" (2018)](http://www.gwvlaw.com/blog/2018/03/nova-scotias-unique-volunteer-protection-legislation.shtml); [Volunteer Emergency Aid Act, RSNB 2016, c 17 (laws.gnb.ca)](https://laws.gnb.ca/en/showdoc/cs/2016-c.17). Confidence high that NB lacks a general act (multiple sources call NS unique), but an exhaustive NB statute sweep wasn't possible — **minor residual UNVERIFIED**.

**Finding 5.2 — Incorporation.** NB nonprofits incorporate under the **Companies Act (NB), Part II** (letters patent; ≥3 incorporators age 19+; NB registered office) via Service New Brunswick Corporate Registry. Incorporation makes the org a separate legal person, shielding members/volunteers/directors from the org's obligations (absent bad faith/personal negligence). No sign NB has replaced this with a modern Not-for-Profit Corporations Act; NB nonprofits may alternatively continue federally under the CNCA.
→ Sources: [GNB — Incorporation of Non-Profit Companies](https://www2.gnb.ca/content/gnb/en/services/services_renderer.201449.Incorporation_of_Non-Profit_Companies.html); [SNB Corporate Registry — Not-for-Profit Companies](https://www2.snb.ca/content/snb/en/sites/corporate-registry/not-for-profit-companies.html); [SNB, "Not-for-Profit Organizations: A Legal Guide" (PDF)](https://www.pxw1.snb.ca/snb7001/e/1000/CSS-FOL-SNB-45-0014E.pdf); [CanadianCharityLaw.ca on NB continuance to CNCA](https://www.canadiancharitylaw.ca/blog/good-news-for-alberta-and-new-brunswick-non-profits-that-wish-to-move-to-the-federal-canada-not-for-profit-corporations-act-cnca/).

**Finding 5.3 — Insurance.** Typical stack for a small community group: **CGL $2M–$5M** (bodily injury/property damage to third parties, incl. volunteer-caused). Cost benchmarks: **~$450/yr for $2M CGL for a small nonprofit** (Canadian broker figure); small orgs generally $400–$2,000/yr. Purpose-built programs exist: **Marsh Canada Non-Profit Community Group (NPCG) program** (up to $5M, optional liquor liability) and **Volunteer Canada/BMS volunteer liability program**. Funders/landlords/municipalities typically require $2M minimum and "additional insured" status. Directors & Officers (D&O) insurance becomes relevant once incorporated with a board.
→ Sources: [getCertain, "Non-Profit Insurance Cost in Canada"](https://getcertain.ca/how-much-does-non-profit-insurance-cost-in-canada/); [Zensurance — Non-Profit Insurance](https://www.zensurance.com/non-profit-insurance); [Marsh Canada NPCG Program](https://www.eventinsurance.marsh.com/ca/en/programs/non-profit-community-group-insurance.html); [BMS/Volunteer Canada Liability Insurance Program (2025 brochure)](https://www.underourwing.bmsgroup.com/downloads/BMS_VCan_Brochure_2025_EN.pdf); [Imagine Canada — Insurance and Liability](https://www.sectorsource.ca/managing-organization/risk-management/insurance-liability). Exact NB quotes will vary — **cost figures are national benchmarks, UNVERIFIED for NB specifically**.

**Actions:** (a) Get CGL quote (Marsh NPCG / Zensurance / local NB broker), $2M minimum — **before-first-pilot**; (b) incorporate (NB Companies Act Part II or federal CNCA) once recurring — **before-scale**; (c) volunteer agreement + role description limiting volunteer improvisation — **before-first-pilot**; (d) D&O policy when a board exists — **before-scale**.

## 6. Photo/video consent at events

**Finding 6.1 — Statutory layer.** NB has **no statutory privacy tort** (unlike BC/SK/MB/NL Privacy Acts) and no private-sector privacy statute; PIPEDA applies only to **commercial activity**. OPC guidance: nonprofit status isn't determinative, but organizing club events, membership lists, newsletters, fundraising are generally *not* commercial activity — so a free civic project's event photos likely fall outside PIPEDA; selling/trading lists or images would change that.
→ Sources: [OPC, "How PIPEDA applies to charitable and non-profit organizations"](https://www.priv.gc.ca/en/privacy-topics/privacy-laws-in-canada/the-personal-information-protection-and-electronic-documents-act-pipeda/r_o_p/02_05_d_19/); [Nelligan Law, "PIPEDA & Non-Profit/Charitable Organizations"](https://nelliganlaw.ca/articles/pipeda-non-profit-charitable-organizations-2/); [Clark Wilson, "Implications of the New Intrusion on Seclusion Tort for BC Organizations"](https://www.cwilson.com/implications-of-the-new-intrusion-on-seclusion-tort-for-bc-organizations/) (lists NB among provinces without a statutory privacy tort).

**Finding 6.2 — Common-law layer.** Ontario recognized intrusion upon seclusion (*Jones v Tsige*, 2012 ONCA 32); NL and NS courts have followed/implicitly recognized privacy torts. **No NB decision adopting these torts was located — NB position UNVERIFIED/undecided**, but an NB court could plausibly follow. Practically: filming in public places is generally lawful (low expectation of privacy), but *Vanderveen v Waterbridge Media* (2017 ONSC) found liability for filming a jogger and using the footage in a **commercial video** without consent — publication/appropriation is the risk, not capture.
→ Sources: [McMillan, "Seclusion Intrusion"](https://mcmillan.ca/insights/seclusion-intrusion-a-common-law-tort-for-invasion-of-privacy/); [All About Information, "Newfoundland court recognizes intrusion upon seclusion" (2022)](https://allaboutinformation.ca/2022/11/28/newfoundland-court-recognizes-intrusion-upon-seclusion-tort/); [Stewart McKelvey, "Court recognizes a new privacy tort for Nova Scotia"](https://stewartmckelvey.com/thought-leadership/careful-what-you-disclose-court-recognizes-a-new-privacy-tort-for-nova-scotia/); [Vilkhov Law, "Is It Illegal to Record Someone Without Consent in Canada?"](https://vilkhovlaw.ca/is-it-illegal-to-record-someone-without-consent-in-canada/) (Vanderveen discussion).

**Finding 6.3 — Norms.** Standard Canadian event practice: written/checkbox media consent at registration (opt-in or opt-out with visual identifier like a lanyard/sticker), signage at gathering points, no publication of identifiable non-consenting participants, prompt takedown on request. Adults-only (18+) removes the minor-consent problem entirely. Bystanders on public sidewalks captured incidentally are low-risk if not featured/exploited commercially.

**Actions:** (a) Add media-consent checkbox (separate from the liability acknowledgment; not a condition of participation) + takedown promise — **before-first-pilot**; (b) simple photo policy for volunteer hosts (no close-ups of non-consenting people; no commercial use) — **before-first-pilot**; (c) revisit PIPEDA if the project ever monetizes (sponsorships using participant images, list-sharing) — **before-scale**.

## 7. City of Fredericton requirements

**Finding 7.1 — Special event permits exist, with long lead times.** The City runs a Special Events application: **minimum 60 working days** before the event, **90 working days if liquor** is to be sold/distributed/consumed on City property. There is also an "Event & Presentation Requests" intake form.
→ Sources: [City of Fredericton — Application for Special Events](https://www.fredericton.ca/recreation-culture/community-connections/application-special-events); [City of Fredericton — Event & Presentation Requests](https://www.fredericton.ca/form/event-presentation-requests). (City pages blocked bots from this environment; details from search extracts. **Insurance/fee specifics for City permits UNVERIFIED — expect a certificate of insurance ~$2M naming the City as additional insured, standard for Canadian municipalities; confirm directly.**)

**Finding 7.2 — Does a 3–6-person walking group need a permit? Almost certainly no — but UNVERIFIED.** No Fredericton bylaw or policy was found imposing permits on small informal gatherings, ordinary sidewalk use, or walking tours; permit regimes target events that reserve space, alter pedestrian/traffic flow, amplify sound, sell things, or serve liquor. A recurring 6-person conversational walk that claims no exclusive space is functionally indistinguishable from friends walking downtown. However, no explicit numeric threshold ("under X people exempt") could be located — **UNVERIFIED; a five-minute email to the City's special-events office would settle it and is strongly recommended** (recurring use of Officers' Square as a fixed meeting point is the one element the City might want to know about).
→ Source basis: absence of contrary provision in [City of Fredericton By-Law Index](https://www.fredericton.ca/en/city-hall/by-laws/by-law-index) search results; Special Events page above.

**Finding 7.3 — Officers' Square: City-managed park with heritage designations.** Officers' Square (Queen/Regent St) is a **Provincial Heritage Place** (designation under NB's Heritage Conservation Act — administered provincially; **statute name inference UNVERIFIED**) and part of the Fredericton Military Compound **National Historic Site (1960)**. Day-to-day it is a **City of Fredericton park**: the City ran the multi-year revitalization, reopened it, and programs its events (concerts, Changing of the Guard, winter rink). So bookings/permissions go through the **City**, not the Province; heritage status matters for structures/alterations, not for standing in the square talking.
→ Sources: [City of Fredericton — Officers' Square](https://www.fredericton.ca/recreation-culture/parks-trails/officers-square); [City of Fredericton — Officers' Square Revitalization](https://www.fredericton.ca/business-development/current-projects/officers-square-revitalization); [City news — Officers' Square Officially Reopens](https://www.fredericton.ca/your-government/news/officers-square-fredericton-officially-reopens-public); [HistoricPlaces.ca — 11 Carleton Street entry](https://www.historicplaces.ca/en/rep-reg/place-lieu.aspx?id=18989); [Attractions Canada — Officers' Square Provincial Heritage Place](https://www.attractionscanada.com/New-Brunswick/Fredericton/Officers-Square-Provincial-Heritage-Place.asp).

**Finding 7.4 — Busking is regulated but irrelevant.** Downtown Fredericton (BIA) + City run a busker **permit program** (Regent↔Westmorland, Queen↔King; audition video; location rotation). It governs *performances for the public*; a language-practice walk isn't busking — but if expeditions ever add street performance elements (songs, skits for passersby), the permit regime would bite.
→ Sources: [Downtown Fredericton — Busking Permits](https://www.downtownfredericton.ca/busking-permits-2021-2/); [Busking Guidelines 2024 (PDF)](https://www.downtownfredericton.ca/wp-content/uploads/2024/04/Busking-Guidelines-2024.pdf); [CBC News, "Fredericton enforces guidelines, permit program for downtown buskers" (2017)](https://www.cbc.ca/news/canada/new-brunswick/downtown-fredericton-guidelines-busker-permits-1.4198183).

**Actions:** (a) Email City special-events office describing the format and asking whether any permission is needed for a recurring ≤6-person meetup starting at Officers' Square (get the answer in writing) — **now**; (b) if the project grows to larger gatherings, signage, tables, or amplified sound at Officers' Square: file Special Events application ≥60 working days ahead (90 if liquor) — **before-scale**; (c) avoid any busking-like activity absent a permit — **now** (policy line in host guide).
