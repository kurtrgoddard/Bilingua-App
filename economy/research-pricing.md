# Behavioral-Science Grounding for Bilingua Quest Pricing — Research Brief (2026-07-14)

## 1. Paid RSVP vs. no-show

**Findings**
- Free events routinely lose **40–60% of registrants to no-shows**, while paid in-person events see **70–90% attendance**; industry data cited by organizers claims free events have a **50%+ higher no-show rate than paid ones** and that "even a $5 registration fee significantly reduces no-shows." Sources: [Glue Up, "How to Reduce Your Event RSVP No-Show Rate"](https://www.glueup.com/blog/fix-high-event-rsvp-no-show-rate); [Tickera organizer guide, 2026](https://blog.tickera.com/reduce-event-no-shows/); [eventcloud](https://www.eventcloud.io/blog/how-to-cut-event-no-shows). These are vendor/organizer blogs, not peer-reviewed — **UNVERIFIED as controlled research**, but the direction is consistent across every industry source.
- Meetup itself introduced a **$2-per-RSVP member fee** (Oct 2024 rollout) explicitly as skin-in-the-game economics — evidence that $2 is treated by the largest community-events platform as the floor for a meaningful commitment. Sources: [WP Tavern](https://wptavern.com/meetup-com-introduces-rsvp-fees-for-members-wordpress-meetup-groups-unaffected-by-pricing-changes); [Romefort, LinkedIn](https://www.linkedin.com/pulse/meetup-charge-2-per-rsvp-attendees-johann-romefort).
- Academic commitment-device literature supports the mechanism: deposit contracts where money is staked and returned on follow-through reliably shift behavior (Giné, Karlan & Zinman 2010 CARES study: deposit contract raised 12-month smoking quit rates ~3.5 pp off an 8.9% base; broader review in Bryan, Karlan & Nelson, *Commitment Devices*, 2010). Sources: [Bryan/Karlan/Nelson PDF](http://houdekpetr.cz/!data/public_html/papers/Bryan%20et%20al%202010.pdf); [Rogers et al., commitment devices in health, JAMA 2014](https://scholar.harvard.edu/files/todd_rogers/files/commitment_devices_2.pdf); [Derksen & Kerwin, appointments as commitment devices, J-PAL WP 2024](https://www.povertyactionlab.org/sites/default/files/research-paper/WP6340_Appointments-a-more-effective-commitment-device-for-health-behaviors-in-Malawi_Kerwin-et-al_Feb2024.pdf). A popular refundable-deposit variant: charge and refund at check-in ([Conferences That Work, June 2026](https://www.conferencesthatwork.com/index.php/event-design/2026/06/reducing-event-no-shows-part-2-beyond-no-show-fees/)).

**Implication for the model.** The single biggest operational risk (a 5-seat expedition where 3 people flake) is directly mitigated by any nonzero price. The threshold is low: $2–5 is where industry practice converges. For the *free* first expedition, the no-show problem returns — use a **refundable card-hold or "free ticket claimed by $5 hold, released at check-in"** to keep commitment without breaking the free promise.

**Recommended parameter.** $5 flat paid seat (well above the $2 floor); first-free seat backed by a $5 refundable hold or at minimum a claimed named ticket + reminder sequence.

## 2. Zero-price effect and free-trial design

**Findings**
- Shampanier, Mazar & Ariely (2007), *Zero as a Special Price*: demand discontinuously jumps at exactly $0 — Lindt truffle at 15¢ vs. Kiss at 1¢: ~73% chose truffle; truffle 14¢ vs. Kiss free: 69% flipped to the Kiss. Free is a category, not a price. Sources: [original paper, Marketing Science 2007](https://web.mit.edu/ariely/www/MIT/Papers/zero.pdf); [Boston Fed WP 06-16](https://www.bostonfed.org/-/media/Documents/Workingpapers/PDF/wp0616.pdf); [Ariely's own caveats](https://danariely.com/the-nuances-of-the-free-experiment/).
- Freemium free-to-paid conversion benchmarks: **~3–5% typical, 6–8% exceptional (median ~4.5%)**; time-limited trials convert far better (**8–25%**), and shorter trials perform as well or better than long ones — a 680,000-user RCT found longer trials boosted adoption 11% but did **not** improve conversion. Sources: [shno.co benchmark roundup, 2026](https://www.shno.co/marketing-statistics/free-trial-conversion-statistics); [PLOS/PMC large-scale trial-duration field experiment](https://pmc.ncbi.nlm.nih.gov/articles/PMC12217587/); [Userpilot](https://userpilot.com/blog/free-trial-length-saas/).
- Gym/studio "first visit free": free trials convert at roughly **20–45%** (with structured follow-up, ~38%; without, ~19%), while *paid* intro offers convert **50–80%** — free trials attract lower-intent users. Sources: [Optimized Growth, 2026](https://optimizedgrowth.com/gyms/blog/free-trial-vs-paid-trial-gym/); [Glofox](https://www.glofox.com/blog/gym-trial-conversion-rates/); [vibefam](https://vibefam.com/what-studio-owners-get-wrong-about-free-trials-and-what-converts-better/). Benchmarks are operator-reported, not academic — **partially UNVERIFIED**.

**Implication.** One free expedition captures nearly all of the zero-price magnetism ("your first expedition is free" is the headline either way); a second and third free session adds free-rider selection, anchors the reference price at $0, and delays the moment of commitment that trial research says should come early. The gym data says the conversion lever is not more free sessions but the **follow-up sequence after session one** (in-game debrief quest, host invite to next expedition, punch card handed over at the café).

**Recommended parameter.** **ONE free expedition** for participants, not three. Build a scripted post-expedition conversion ritual. Expect 20–40% free-to-paid conversion if follow-up is structured; treat <15% as a design failure, not a pricing failure.

## 3. Price-point psychology at ~$5

**Findings**
- **Round vs. charm pricing:** Wadhwa & Zhang (2015, *Journal of Consumer Research*, "This Number Just Feels Right") — rounded prices ($5.00) are processed fluently and increase purchase intent for **hedonic/emotional** purchases; non-rounded ($4.87 or $4.99) fit utilitarian evaluation. A social French adventure is squarely hedonic. Sources: [paper PDF](https://www.smallprojectsbureau.com/wp-content/uploads/2020/01/wadhwa-zhang-2015.pdf); [ScienceDaily summary](https://www.sciencedaily.com/releases/2015/01/150122084344.htm); [Psychology Today](https://www.psychologytoday.com/us/blog/consumed/201410/the-price-feels-right).
- **Coffee anchoring:** the average latte in Canada passed **$5 CAD** (Square Canada data, 2024: avg $5.19; Starbucks Canada lattes now ~$5.65–$7.75). "$5 — the price of one latte — for 45 minutes of real French" is a congruent, pre-anchored frame, especially since the event *ends at a café*. Sources: [Square Canada press data](https://squareup.com/ca/en/press/coffee-data-canada); [VITA Daily, 2024](https://vitamagazine.com/2024/05/27/latte-price-surpasses-5-in-canada-heres-what-you-need-to-know-about-your-daily-cuppa/); [Starbucks Canada menu tracker 2026](https://starbucks-menus.ca/coffee-menu/) (menu trackers UNVERIFIED as official).
- **PWYW vs. fixed low price:** Gneezy, Gneezy, Nelson & Brown (2010, *Science*, N=113,047 theme-park photos): PWYW alone → mass uptake but ~$0.92 average payment (barely break-even); fixed price + charity → almost no lift; **PWYW + 50%-to-charity ("shared social responsibility") → dramatically more profitable** (average payments reported around $5.33 — figure widely reported in secondary coverage; exact number **UNVERIFIED** against the paper text here). Museum PWYW (Philadelphia Museum of Art trial) lifted attendance 128% and revenue 20% — good for access, bad for predictable per-seat revenue. Sources: [Science 329:325](https://www.science.org/doi/10.1126/science.1186744); [paper PDF (Wharton mirror)](https://marketing.wharton.upenn.edu/wp-content/uploads/2016/10/Gneezy20Gneezy20Nelson2020Brown202010.pdf); [National Geographic coverage](https://www.nationalgeographic.com/science/article/caring-with-cash-or-how-radiohead-could-have-made-more-money); [WHYY on PMA](https://whyy.org/articles/philadelphia-art-museum-pay-what-you-wish-tickets/). Radiohead's In Rainbows grossed ~$3M via PWYW but with huge variance ([Medium case study](https://binodpanda66.medium.com/radiohead-case-study-pay-what-you-want-pwyw-strategy-622569d29ac8)).

**Implication.** Fixed **$5.00 even** beats both $4.99 (wrong mindset for a hedonic social purchase) and pure PWYW (revenue collapses toward ~$1 and hosts can't be paid predictably). If you ever want an access valve, offer a quiet "sponsored seat" (see §6) rather than PWYW.

**Recommended parameter.** **$5.00 CAD flat, round, framed against the latte** ("one latte = one expedition"). Optional PWYW-above-floor tip line at checkout, not PWYW pricing.

## 4. Punch cards / bundles

**Findings**
- **Endowed-progress effect** (Nunes & Drèze 2006, *JMR*, car-wash field study): a 10-stamp card **pre-stamped with 2** was completed by 34% vs. 19% for an 8-stamp blank card requiring identical effort — and completers bought faster. Effect requires a stated *reason* for the head start (even an arbitrary one). Sources: [Coglode summary](https://www.coglode.com/nuggets/endowed-progress-effect); [Learning Loop](https://learningloop.io/plays/psychology/endowed-progress-effect); [Knowledge@Wharton](https://knowledge.wharton.upenn.edu/article/the-lowdown-on-customer-loyalty-programs-which-are-the-most-effective-and-why/).
- **Studio pack norms:** 10-class packs are conventionally priced **10–20% below drop-in** (e.g., $120 pack vs. $15 drop-in), with a **1–3 month expiry** to protect cash flow and drive frequency. Sources: [OfferingTree](https://www.offeringtree.com/blog/setting-the-best-price-for-your-group-classes/); [Gymdesk](https://gymdesk.com/blog/how-to-price-your-yoga-classes-for-success/); [Mindbody](https://www.mindbodyonline.com/business/education/blog/are-you-pricing-your-yoga-classes-correctly).

**Implication.** $40 for 10 seats is a 20% discount — at the top of the normal discount range (fine for a community product), and it converts ten fragile per-event decisions into one committed prepayment (also slashing payment-processing drag, see §8). Two behavioral upgrades: (1) make the punch card a **12-slot card with 2 slots pre-stamped** — one stamped "your free first expedition," one stamped "founders' bonus" — the reason-giving Nunes & Drèze require; (2) stamp it physically at the café for ritual value.

**Recommended parameter.** **$40 / 10 expeditions ($4/seat), 6-month expiry**, presented as a 12-slot card with 2 pre-stamped. Reward for completion: a free "graduation" expedition or in-game civic badge.

## 5. Marketplace take rates

**Findings** (all standard published rates unless flagged)
- Airbnb homes: moved to a **15.5% host-only fee** (Oct 2025) ([mostlymetrics take-rate comparison](https://www.mostlymetrics.com/p/comparing-marketplace-take-rates)); **Airbnb Experiences: flat 20% host commission** ([Regiondo](https://pro.regiondo.com/blog/airbnb-experiences-host/); [Xola](https://www.xola.com/articles/what-are-airbnb-experiences/)); tour OTAs Viator/GetYourGuide **20–30%**, and GYG's creep past 30% is actively driving guide defection ([SambaHQ OTA commission guide 2026](https://www.sambahq.com/ota-supplier-guide/ota-commission-rates); [Guidz](https://guidzapp.com/airbnb-experiences-complaints-alternative/)).
- Uber mobility take rate ≈ high-20s–30% of gross bookings (2025 gross bookings $193B per [SEC filings](https://www.sec.gov/Archives/edgar/data/1543151/000154315126000015/uber-20251231.htm); exact segment take rate **UNVERIFIED** here — commonly reported ~28–30%).
- Eventbrite (Canada): **3.5% + C$1.29/ticket + 2.9% processing** — effective ~15–20% take on a $10 ticket, brutal on a $5 one ([Checkout Page fee breakdown 2026](https://checkoutpage.com/blog/eventbrite-fees); [Eventbrite pricing](https://www.eventbrite.com/organizer/pricing/)). Meetup: SaaS model, organizer pays **~$17–30/mo standard** ([Meetup help](https://help.meetup.com/hc/en-us/articles/28677808413197-Organizer-Subscription-prices-overview)) plus the $2 RSVP fee experiment.
- Commentary consensus: two-sided marketplace default **10–20%**; platforms creep to 25–30% only when they supply demand the seller couldn't get alone; above that, supplier resentment and multi-homing set in ([mostlymetrics, "Mostly Middlemen"](https://www.mostlymetrics.com/p/mostly-marketplaces-how-the-middleman); [Deliberate Directions, "Why 15% kills some platforms while others thrive at 30%"](https://deliberatedirections.com/why-most-platform-businesses-get-revenue-share-wrong/)).

**Implication.** Your proposed platform slice is 25% + a 15% fund = hosts keep 60%. Airbnb Experiences hosts keep 80%; even Viator guides keep ~75%. For **semi-volunteer, mission-motivated hosts**, the raw percentage matters less than at commercial platforms, but 60% is below every comparable — risky for the "fair-feeling" test *unless* the 15% community fund is framed as the hosts' own collective asset (materials they use, scholarships they award), not a platform levy. At these dollar amounts (a full 6-seat paid event grosses $30), the honest framing is honorarium + cause, not gig income: 60% of $30 = $18/event ≈ $24/hr equivalent for the 45 min — actually respectable in Fredericton *if* events fill.

**Recommended parameter.** **Host 65% / platform 20% / community fund 15%**, computed net of payment-processing fees, with the fund governed by a host council. 20% matches the Airbnb Experiences anchor; the fund is presented as "hosts' fund," not platform revenue. Revisit only above ~$100k GMV.

## 6. Prosocial pricing

**Findings**
- Dunn, Aknin & Norton (2008, *Science*): spending on others → higher happiness; effect replicated cross-culturally and strongest when giving satisfies **relatedness** and the recipient is concrete. Caveat: the original lab experiment was small (N=46, p=.042) and a 2022 direct replication (N=133) was mixed on the primary measure. Sources: [HBS/Science paper](https://www.hbs.edu/ris/download.aspx?name=Does+Spending+Money+on+Others+Promote+Happiness.pdf); [Dunn/Aknin/Norton 2014 review, Current Directions](https://journals.sagepub.com/doi/abs/10.1177/0963721413512503); [PLOS ONE replication, 2022](https://journals.plos.org/plosone/article?id=10.1371/journal.pone.0272434) — treat as directionally supported, effect size modest.
- **Shared social responsibility** (Gneezy et al. 2010, §3 above) is the strongest evidence that earmarking half a price for others **raises willingness to pay and total profit** when buyers have agency over the payment.
- **Caffè sospeso**: a century-old Neapolitan norm of buying a "suspended" coffee for an anonymous stranger; revived post-2008 and spread globally, **including Canada**. It is a ready-made cultural script for "your $5 also buys a stranger's first coffee." Sources: [Wikipedia](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Caff%C3%A8_sospeso); [Eataly](https://www.eataly.com/us_en/magazine/culture-and-tradition/caffe-sospeso); [Fodor's on Caffè Gambrinus](https://www.fodors.com/world/europe/italy/naples/experiences/news/this-generous-cafe-tradition-lives-on-in-naples-even-despite-covid).
- Direct evidence that earmarking increases WTP at a *fixed* price is weaker — Gneezy's fixed-price+charity condition barely moved demand. The lift comes when the buyer feels agency. **UNVERIFIED** that "$5 with visible slice for others" outsells plain $5.

**Implication.** Don't rely on the earmark to raise conversion at $5 (fixed-price + charity was Gneezy's weakest cell). Instead deploy prosociality where the research says it works: (a) an **optional sospeso add-on** at checkout ("+$5: suspend a first coffee/expedition for a newcomer") — agency-based, sospeso-scripted, funds the free-first-expedition pool; (b) make the 15% fund's outputs visible and concrete (photos of the conversation-card decks it printed).

**Recommended parameter.** Keep the base price clean at $5; add an optional **$5 "expedition suspendue"** line item; publicly report fund spending quarterly.

## 7. Host apprenticeship (unpaid initiation before paid status)

**Findings**
- **Platform norms:** Airbnb Experiences human-reviews every submission (2–4 weeks, waitlists in dense markets), requires identity verification, ~2 years relevant experience/credentials, and rejects listings lacking distinctiveness — vetting by portfolio, not by unpaid trial events. Sources: [Airbnb Help: quality standards](https://www.airbnb.com/help/article/1451/what-are-the-quality-standards-for-airbnb-experiences); [Airbnb Help: how vetting works](https://www.airbnb.com/help/article/3901); [Uplisting guide](https://www.uplisting.io/blog/airbnb-experiences-host-guide). I found **no direct empirical study** showing unpaid probationary events filter for gig quality/commitment — **UNVERIFIED as a tested mechanism**; it is, however, the standard structure in volunteer sectors (docent training, ESL conversation-circle facilitation).
- **Psychology:** Aronson & Mills (1959), effort justification — costlier initiation increases subsequent liking of and commitment to the group; the apprenticeship itself plausibly *builds* host loyalty, not just screens for it. Source: [original paper PDF](https://web.mit.edu/curhan/www/docs/Articles/15341_Readings/Motivation/Aronson_Mills_1959_The_effect_of_severity_of_initiation.pdf); [BPS retrospective](https://www.bps.org.uk/psychologist/initiation-ceremony-experiments).

**Implication.** Three free events is a real cost (~3 evenings) — enough initiation to trigger effort-justification bonding and self-selection of the mission-driven, without the credentialism of Airbnb. Risk: losing good hosts who can't afford unpaid work. Mitigate by making apprenticeship events *count* — endowed progress again: apprentice events pre-stamp the host's own progression track, and apprentices co-host (shadow a senior Hôte) rather than run rooms alone.

**Recommended parameter.** Keep **3 apprenticeship events**, restructured as 1 shadow + 2 co-hosted, with expenses (coffee) covered from the community fund, and automatic "Hôte confirmé" status + retroactive small bonus (e.g., first paid event pays 75%) on completion.

## 8. Canadian practicalities

**Findings**
- **GST/HST small-supplier threshold: $30,000** in worldwide taxable supplies over four consecutive calendar quarters (or in a single quarter → immediate loss of status; register within 29 days). Below it, no registration or charging required; voluntary registration allows input-tax-credit recovery. New Brunswick HST is 15%, so crossing the line turns $5.00 into $5.75 or compresses margin ~13%. Sources: [CRA: When to register](https://www.canada.ca/en/revenue-agency/services/tax/businesses/topics/gst-hst-businesses/when-register-charge.html); [CRA: Small suppliers](https://www.canada.ca/en/revenue-agency/services/forms-publications/publications/2-2/small-suppliers.html); [Insight CPA explainer 2026](https://insightscpa.ca/qa-hst-threshold-small-suppliers/). At $5/seat, $30k = 6,000 paid seats/yr (~23 full events *per week*) — not a near-term concern; individual hosts earning ~$15–20/event are nowhere close.
- **Stripe Canada: 2.9% + C$0.30 domestic** (2026; +0.8% international cards, +2% FX). On a **$5 charge the fee is $0.445 → 8.9%**; on the **$40 pack it's $1.46 → 3.65%**. Sources: [Stripe Canada pricing](https://stripe.com/en-ca/pricing); [affonso.io CAD calculator 2026](https://affonso.io/resources/stripe-fee-calculator/canada). Eventbrite would be worse on $5 tickets (fixed C$1.29 + percentages, §5).

**Implication.** Single $5 Stripe charges are *practical but wasteful*; the punch card cuts processing drag by 5+ points, and per-seat charges should be batched (charge at RSVP, or hold-and-capture at check-in — which doubles as the §1 commitment device). Stay a small supplier deliberately; structure host payouts as monthly aggregated transfers (Stripe Connect or Interac e-Transfer) to avoid per-event payout fees.

**Recommended parameter.** Push the $40 pack as the default purchase ("most explorers carry the card"); use auth-hold + capture-at-check-in for singles; monthly host payouts; monitor the $30k line annually.

## Recommended model (stress-tested)

| Parameter | Proposed | Recommended | Rationale (key evidence) |
|---|---|---|---|
| Free participant expeditions | 1 | **1**, with $5 refundable hold to claim | Zero-price effect fully captured at one (Shampanier/Ariely); more free = free-rider selection + $0 anchor (gym trial data); hold fixes free-event no-shows (40–60% baseline) |
| Per-seat price | ~$5 | **$5.00 CAD flat, round** | Hedonic round-price fluency (Wadhwa & Zhang); latte anchor >$5 in Canada (Square); above Meetup's $2 commitment floor; PWYW rejected (Gneezy avg $0.92) |
| Pack | 10 for ~$40 | **$40 / 10 seats ($4/seat, 20% off), sold as 12-slot card with 2 pre-stamped, 6-mo expiry** | Studio norm 10–20% discount; endowed progress lifts completion 19%→34% (Nunes & Drèze); cuts Stripe drag from 8.9% to 3.65% |
| Revenue split | 60 / 25 / 15 | **Host 65 / platform 20 / fund 15** (net of processing), fund host-governed | Airbnb Experiences anchor = 20% platform; 60% host keep is below all comparables; fund framed as hosts' collective asset, spending published |
| Prosocial layer | implicit | **Optional +$5 "expedition suspendue" add-on** funding newcomers' free seats/coffees | Earmarking works with agency (Gneezy SSR), not baked into fixed price; caffè sospeso is the ready-made script; Dunn/Aknin warm-glow (modest, replicated directionally) |
| Host apprenticeship | 3 free events | **3 events: 1 shadow + 2 co-hosted, expenses covered, retroactive first-event bonus** | Effort justification builds commitment (Aronson & Mills); matches volunteer-sector norms; no direct gig-quality RCT exists (flagged) — so lower the cash cost, keep the effort cost |
| Minimum viable paid event | — | **4 paid seats** → host earns $13 (65% × $20) ≈ $17/hr for 45 min; guarantee a **$12 host floor** from the fund if 3 show | Keeps every run event honorarium-positive; auth-holds make 4 committed seats realistic given paid-event 70–90% attendance |
| Payments/tax | Stripe | Stripe, hold-and-capture at check-in; monthly aggregated host payouts; stay under **$30k** GST/HST small-supplier line (= ~6,000 paid seats/yr) | Stripe CA 2.9%+$0.30; NB HST 15% cliff at $30k over 4 quarters (CRA) |

**Biggest open risks (flagged UNVERIFIED):** the "$5 cuts flaking" magnitude comes from vendor blogs, not RCTs — instrument your own no-show data from day one; the fixed-price prosocial framing ("your $5 buys a stranger's coffee") has *no* evidence of lifting conversion and Gneezy's fixed+charity cell suggests it may do nothing — keep it as an opt-in add-on and A/B it; host supply at ~$13–18/event depends on mission motivation, not economics, so the apprenticeship's bonding function and the fund's visibility are load-bearing.
