# Bilingua — The Second Conversation

*A strategy for dusting it off. July 2026.*

*Every statistic and market claim in this document is sourced and verification-flagged in [STRATEGY-SOURCES.md](STRATEGY-SOURCES.md).*

---

## The verdict, up front

**Yes, it's worth dusting off — but not by rebuilding the app.** The 2025 build was an app in search of a community. The 2026 move is a community that eventually earns an app.

Four things changed since you shelved it, and all four break in your favour:

1. **The problem got measured.** Statistics Canada data released in 2024 quantified exactly what you intuited: New Brunswick has the *highest* French-immersion participation in Canada and one of the worst conversion rates into actual bilingual adults. The gap between "learned French" and "speaks French" is now a documented national embarrassment with your province at its centre.
2. **The obvious partner died.** Dialogue NB — the 30-year-old organization whose founding mandate was bridging NB's two language communities — drifted from that mandate in 2018, rebranded as Cohesia in 2023, and ceased operations that September. There is now a *vacuum* where anglophone–francophone rapprochement work used to live, and two government grant programs that exist specifically to fund it.
3. **AI made rehearsal free — which makes the human conversation scarce and valuable.** A wave of AI speaking-practice apps (Speak is a $1B company now) is training thousands of people to talk to robots. None of them can supply the thing your app was always really about: a real person, real stakes, across a real table. AI is not your competitor. AI is your funnel.
4. **The technology to build it became trivial.** What took you months on Replit in 2025 is now weeks of work. But — and this is the whole argument below — the code was never the hard part, and it still isn't.

The honest commercial read: **as a venture-scale business, no. As a grant-funded civic project with a real commercial option later, genuinely yes.** The recommendation is gift-first, one city, one café, one ritual — details in [The money](#the-money) and [The smallest real test](#the-smallest-real-test).

## The gap was never French

Here is the problem, in numbers (all sourced in the appendix):

- New Brunswick is Canada's only officially bilingual province. **34%** of its people can hold a conversation in both languages — a number that has been *flat for twenty years*.
- That 34% is not symmetric. About **73% of francophone New Brunswickers are bilingual. About 16% of anglophones are.** Bilingualism in NB is overwhelmingly labour performed by one of the two communities.
- It is not for lack of trying. **37.3%** of NB's anglophone-sector kids are or were in French immersion — the highest rate in the country. The province's own data says 99% of students who *finish* immersion reach conversational oral proficiency (B1+).
- And then it evaporates. Among NB adults 25–54 who went through immersion, only about **60% can still hold a conversation in French**. Nationally it's worse: barely half of former immersion students retain conversational French. A Toronto-area study found **67% of immersion graduates reported not using French at all** after graduation.

Put those together and you get the strangest market in Canadian education: **New Brunswick produces conversational French speakers at the highest rate in the country and then loses them to silence.** The province spends twelve years and (as of the September 2025 federal-provincial agreement) $133.6 million over four years building the skill — and then provides *nothing* between graduation and the grave to use it. Mauril, the federal government's $16M language app, trains listening comprehension. The free settlement-agency French classes exclude Canadian citizens by design. The pipeline ends at the exact moment the skill either gets used or dies.

Why do they go silent? The language-anxiety literature has known for forty years. Foreign-language anxiety is a distinct, situation-specific fear — roughly **a third of learners** experience it at meaningful levels, speaking is its worst trigger, and it measurably degrades performance at every cognitive stage. The most useful frame comes from Peter MacIntyre (a professor at Cape Breton University, fittingly): what predicts whether a person actually speaks a second language is not competence but **willingness to communicate** — a moment-to-moment decision governed by anxiety, perceived competence, and the social setting. "The ultimate goal of language education," MacIntyre and colleagues wrote in 1998, "should be to engender willingness to communicate."

And there's a second mechanism, the one every NB anglophone learner knows in their body: **the switch.** You attempt French; the francophone hears the accent or the hesitation and flips to English — out of politeness, out of efficiency, sometimes out of self-protection of their own language. The Commissioner of Official Languages' 2024 focus groups documented both sides of it: learners frustrated that their French "isn't good enough," francophones admitting they "switch to English too easily to accommodate anglophones." Every switch is a tiny verdict: *your French isn't worth my patience.* Twelve years of immersion, and the graduate's actual lived experience of speaking French is a series of small public failures that end in English.

**So the diagnosis: New Brunswick does not have a French-education problem. It has a French-*permission* problem.** The skill exists, latent, in tens of thousands of adults. What's missing is a social setting in which using it badly is safe, sanctioned, and expected.

That is not a problem you solve with an app that teaches. It's a problem you solve with alchemy.

## The alchemy

Rory Sutherland's core claim in *Alchemy* is that the biggest gains come not from changing the thing but from changing what the thing *means* — that humans run on psycho-logic, not logic, and that "the opposite of a good idea can be another good idea." Applied here, six reframes. These are the strategy.

### 1. You are not fixing a skill deficit. You are lowering a social price.

Every language product on earth sells improvement: more vocabulary, better grammar, higher streaks. But your users already *have* the French — the census proves it. What they can't afford is the **status cost of speaking it badly in public**. Logic says: practice more, improve, then speak. Psycho-logic says: nobody practices a thing that humiliates them, so the improvement never comes.

Sutherland's move is to stop paying the improvement bill and start paying the *embarrassment* bill, which is far cheaper. Everything below is a mechanism for making bad French socially expensive to *mock* and socially safe to *speak*. The product is not education. The product is permission.

### 2. The L-plate insight: build the permission structure, not the platform

Sutherland is obsessed with what he calls alibis and permission structures — people often want to do something and need only a socially acceptable excuse. Consider the learner driver. A new driver is objectively dangerous and slow, and yet nobody rages at them — because there's a plate on the car. **The L-plate converts incompetence into a protected social status.** Everyone who sees it knows the contract: this person is learning; adjust your expectations; do not honk.

Spoken language has no L-plate. A learner opens their mouth and is judged by fluent-adult standards instantly — and the francophone, receiving no signal that patience is wanted, does the polite-efficient thing and switches.

**Bilingua's most valuable product may not be software. It may be an object.** A pin, a coaster, a table card in partner cafés:

> **« Ici, on pratique. »** — *We're practicing. S'il vous plaît, ne changez pas de langue — please don't switch.*

That object does three jobs at once: it licenses the anglophone to be bad (the plate is on the car); it licenses the francophone to *stay in French* — which solves the single biggest failure mode of real-world practice, because now staying in French is honouring a request rather than watching someone drown; and it makes the private act of practicing publicly visible (see [How it spreads](#how-it-spreads)). The app, whenever it returns, is the thing that gets two people to the table. The coaster is the thing that changes what happens *at* the table.

### 3. Kill the coffee debt. Institute the moitié-moitié.

The 2025 build contains one line of copy that quietly poisons the whole social contract. From the signup terms:

> *"Note: Language learners are expected to offer to buy coffee for native speakers as a courtesy."*

Look at what that sentence *means*, psycho-logically. It frames the learner as a **debtor** and the francophone as a **service provider**. It makes the conversation charity, purchased with a coffee. Charity is precisely the frame that produces shame on one side and obligation-fatigue on the other — and obligation-fatigue is why the supply side of every language exchange dries up.

Replace it with a ritual: **the moitié-moitié.** One coffee's worth of conversation, split by a hard rule — half in French, half in English. Not negotiable, not "whatever feels natural" (what feels natural is the switch). When the halfway mark hits, the languages flip.

The alchemy is **symmetric vulnerability**. For half the encounter, the anglophone is the incompetent one; for the other half, the francophone — who very often wants to polish professional English (NB's bilingual-services economy runs on it) — takes their turn stumbling. Embarrassment, evenly distributed, stops being shame and becomes camaraderie. It's the difference between visiting a tutor and being lab partners. And note the Sutherland signature: you don't *remove* the awkwardness (impossible), you **redistribute it until it changes meaning.**

This format is proven, incidentally — just never in a small market. Franglish has run bar-based speed exchanges on exactly this structure (seven minutes French, seven minutes English, switch) in Paris, London, Toronto and a dozen other cities for over a decade. The format works. What Franglish never solved is liquidity below ~1M population — which is what the rest of this document is for.

*(A happy accident worth keeping: « moitié-moitié » is also how you'd order a half-and-half. The ritual is named after a drink. Rituals named after drinks spread.)*

### 4. Minimize the worst case, not the average

Sutherland's McDonald's argument: people don't choose the best option, they choose the one that can't be terrible. Variance-reduction beats optimization in almost all human choices — especially first-time, socially risky ones.

Nobody's first Bilingua conversation fails because it wasn't *optimal*. It fails in imagination, weeks earlier, when they picture the worst case: trapped for an open-ended hour with a stranger, drowning in Chiac they can't follow, no script, no exit. So the design brief is not "make the best conversation possible." It's **make the worst possible conversation clearly survivable**:

- **Time-boxed.** Thirty minutes, stated up front. A bad thirty minutes is an anecdote; a bad open-ended coffee is a trauma.
- **Scripted.** A conversation menu on the table card — three questions per language, seeded from the pair's shared interests. Silence is the worst case; the card makes silence impossible.
- **Public and repeatable.** Partner café, staff who know the program, other pairs visible at other tables. (The 2025 build's safety instincts — public venues only, meetup proposals, report tooling — were correct. Keep all of it.)
- **Pre-rehearsed.** See reframe 5.

### 5. AI is the rehearsal room. The café is the performance. (Or: avoid the doorman fallacy.)

Sutherland's *doorman fallacy*: a hotel that redefines the doorman as "someone who opens the door," automates the door, and fires him — destroying the greeting, the status, the taxi-hailing, the security, everything the doorman actually was. The AI language apps are committing the doorman fallacy at scale: they've redefined conversation as "producing comprehensible utterances," automated that, and deleted the human — the stakes, the warmth, the face. The emerging critique of these apps says it plainly: learners who only practice in the zero-stakes room freeze in the real world, because the entire skill being avoided — performing under mild social risk — is the skill.

So refuse to compete with Speak and Duolingo Max, and refuse to ignore them. **Use them as the on-ramp.** The 2026-trivial build is an AI warm-up partner that rehearses *the actual upcoming meeting*: same conversation menu, same café script, your real partner's first name, even Acadian speech patterns so school-French ears aren't ambushed by Chiac. You walk in having already had this conversation once, badly, in private, at zero stakes.

The positioning line writes itself and doubles as a category attack:

> **Practice with a robot so you can talk to a person.**

Every dollar those companies spend teaching the world that speaking practice matters is a dollar spent marketing your café table. They create the rehearsed; you own the performance.

### 6. Pay the supply side in status, not coffee

The hard side of this network is francophones willing to give learners their time — every program in NB implicitly competes for them. The 2025 build paid them in coffee (charity frame — see reframe 3). Money would be worse: paying turns a warm relational act into a badly paid gig (and italki already owns the paid-tutor market).

Sutherland: when you can't pay in money, pay in **meaning**. Make the francophone role a *named, visible, scarce honour* — **Hôte** — with the insignia to match: the pin, first access to events, name on the café's wall, a Lieutenant-Governor-adjacent award at year's end if the institutional partnerships mature (NB loves an official honour; the vice-regal Dialogue Award precedent existed). Costly signaling requires the signal to be *earned*: cap the number of Hôtes per city. A cap on a free role is absurd by logic and perfect by psycho-logic — scarcity is what converts a favour into a title.

And build the **graduation flywheel**: the app's explicit win condition for a learner is not retention, it's *not needing it anymore* — at which point the anglophone is invited to flip roles and host English-side conversations for francophones. Yesterday's demand becomes tomorrow's supply. An app whose KPI is conversations-had rather than daily-active-users is a strange, trust-generating thing (and, per Made to Stick, *unexpectedness* is an asset: "the app that wants you to leave" is a story journalists write about for free).

## The product this implies

Strategy-level only (no build plan here — that's a separate decision for a separate day). The shape that falls out of the six reframes:

**The atomic unit is one good first conversation, not a chat relationship.** The 2025 build was architected around ongoing messaging — matching into an open-ended chat, like every app that dies of ghosting (HelloTalk's documented core failure: "you match, chat for two days, then they stop replying"). Invert it. Everything funnels toward a scheduled, structured, thirty-minute moitié-moitié at a partner café. Chat exists only to arrange it.

**Events before matching; matching as follow-up.** Cold-start truth from every precedent (Franglish, Mundo Lingo, the timebanking literature): recurring anchor events beat 1:1 matching in thin markets, because an event batches supply and demand into one room at one time. **Bilingua Nights** — monthly, one café, Franglish-format rounds, coasters on every table — are the matchmaker. The app's job is the *aftermath*: "you and Marie-Claude clicked; here are three times you're both free next week, here's your conversation menu."

**The AI warm-up is the one genuinely new build.** Rehearse-your-actual-meeting (reframe 5). It's the 2026 capability that didn't exist for the 2025 build, it de-fangs the scariest moment in the funnel, and it's the feature no global app can replicate *locally* (an AI that warms you up for Chiac and knows your café is a moat exactly as wide as your smallness).

**Institutional cohorts, not consumer signups.** The Ten Thousand Coffees playbook — the one hyperlocal-matching model with a proven Canadian track record — sells through institutions that enroll whole cohorts. NB is unusually rich in them: the provincial government itself (roughly 40% of GNB positions carry a bilingual requirement — HR departments have *budgets* for this), the universities (UNB, STU, Mount Allison's student-run French circle, Université de Moncton's bridge programs), the public libraries (already hosting conversation circles, already promoting government language apps, free venues in every town), and Canadian Parents for French NB (which already runs "conversational opportunities for parents"). One employer cohort plus one café is an atomic network.

**Keep the region-unlock mechanic.** It was the best growth idea in the 2025 build and it's *more* right now: cities unlock when enough French-side and English-side members join (the 50/50 split bars were exactly correct), which converts the cold-start problem into a visible civic scoreboard — Moncton vs. Fredericton is a rivalry you can print in a newspaper.

## How it spreads

Jonah Berger's STEPPS, mapped honestly — including where the 2025 concept had gaps:

- **Social currency.** In NB, *becoming actually bilingual* is high-status identity — the province's founding aspiration, achieved by only 16% of anglophones. People share what makes them look good: "I'm doing my French over coffee with an Acadian from Dieppe" is a better story at a dinner party than any streak. Sell the identity, not the utility. The pin/coaster is wearable social currency; the **Hôte** title is social currency for the supply side.
- **Triggers.** The genius of the coffee anchor: **every café in the province becomes an involuntary reminder.** Coffee is a daily trigger; "language app" is not. The physical presence in partner cafés (coasters, table cards, a small shelf sign) means the trigger fires even for people who've never installed anything.
- **Emotion.** The dominant emotions here are high-arousal and real: the *pride* of finally using twelve dormant years, and the low-grade *indignation* of the stat that produces it (all that immersion, ending in silence). Awe-adjacent human stories — the 55-year-old who ordered in French for the first time since 1989 — are the sharable atom.
- **Public.** The 2025 app was invisible: private chats, private meetups. The 2026 design is deliberately visible — branded tables at cafés, pins on lapels, Bilingua Nights that passers-by can watch through the window, a public regional scoreboard. Made to observe, made to imitate.
- **Practical value.** Bilingual fluency has hard cash value in NB — a $1.4B bilingual-services export industry, 15,000+ jobs, and that ~40% bilingual-requirement figure in provincial hiring. "Practical value" content (what bilingualism adds to an NB paycheque) is the shareable utility layer for the employer/cohort channel.
- **Stories.** The founding story is already a Trojan horse: *the province that teaches French better than anywhere in Canada and then never speaks it — and the app that fixed it with a coaster.* Local media will not need persuading; the immersion wars of 2022–23 (a minister resigning, 13,000 people at consultations, a premier's plan scrapped in ten weeks) kept this wound on NB's front pages for years, and the current premier is herself an immersion graduate. This is a province primed to want a hopeful chapter in that story.

## What we say

Made to Stick (SUCCESs: Simple, Unexpected, Concrete, Credible, Emotional, Stories) applied to the message platform. Three candidates were stress-tested; the recommendation is Platform A with B as the ritual's name.

**Platform A — the lead message:**

> **You studied French for twelve years. Have one conversation.**
> « Douze ans de français à l'école. Une vraie conversation. »

- *Simple:* the entire strategy in nine words — the product is one conversation, not an education.
- *Unexpected:* every competitor promises fluency, streaks, progress. Promising *one conversation* breaks the pattern — and quietly indicts the twelve years.
- *Concrete:* countable years, one countable coffee. No abstraction ("connect," "journey," "unlock") survives.
- *Credible:* the census data backs the implicit accusation; the 99%-reach-B1-then-lose-it arc makes it statistically honest.
- *Emotional:* names the reader's private shame gently, then offers absolution at the price of one coffee.
- *Story:* it's a narrative arc in two sentences — wasted past, redeemable present.

**Platform B — the ritual/product name:** **Moitié-moitié.** *Half your coffee in French. Half in English. Nobody's fluent, everybody's brave.* Use it the way "designated driver" was used: a named social role and rule that people can invoke without explanation. Naming the behaviour is what makes the behaviour real.

**Platform C — the category attack (for the AI moment):** *Practice with a robot so you can talk to a person.* Reserve for press, partnerships, and any moment the AI-tutor wave needs judo-ing.

One discipline note: the 2025 copy ("Start Your Journey," "Premier Language Exchange Platform," "rich cultural heritage") is category-generic — it could be any app, anywhere. Every retained line should pass a simple test: *could Tandem say this?* If yes, cut it.

## Viability, honestly

**The market math.** NB has ~775–800K people. Generously: ~560K anglophones, of whom ~37% have immersion exposure — call it a theoretical pool of 100–150K adults with latent French, of whom realistically a low single-digit percentage are reachable and motivated in the first years. This is a **thousands-of-users** proposition, not millions. No venture story exists here, and pretending otherwise would poison the honest version. What does exist: a civic project of real significance, a fundable nonprofit program, and — if the model proves out — a replicable playbook (see money section) for every bilingual border region in the world.

**The two-sided cold start is the real risk.** Hyperlocal marketplaces die of empty rooms. Mitigations, in order of strength: anchor events (batch liquidity), institutional cohorts (buy liquidity wholesale via employers/universities/libraries), region-locking (concentrate everything in one city until it's undeniably alive — the 2025 unlock mechanic, kept), and the graduation flywheel (grow supply from within). The timebanking literature's warning applies with full force: volunteer-coordinated exchange systems collapse when the coordinator burns out or the grant lapses. Whatever gets built must be boring-cheap to operate.

**The competitive map, compressed.** Global exchange apps (Tandem ~18M users, HelloTalk ~70M claimed) are alive, online-only, and structurally mismatched — their documented failure modes (ghosting within a week, dating-app drift, harassment complaints, zero local density, no in-person layer) are precisely Bilingua's design brief. The AI wave is complementary (reframe 5). The public stack (Mauril, Explore bursaries, newcomer classes) stops at comprehension or excludes citizens. In-person NB offerings are paid classes and drop-in circles — **nothing in the province does matched, reciprocal, structured 1:1 exchange.** The niche is genuinely, verifiably empty. It's empty partly because it's hard (thin market) — but the *reasons* it's hard are exactly the things grants, institutions, and rituals solve better than venture software does.

**Why the constraints are the moat.** A global app can never credibly do coffee in Moncton: can't know the cafés, can't run the Nights, can't earn the libraries' trust, can't tune an AI to Chiac, can't win a Lieutenant-Governor's award. Smallness — fatal to the venture version — is the *entire defensibility* of the civic version. (Sutherland again: the opposite of a good idea. "Too small a market" inverts into "a market only you can love.")

## The money

**Recommendation: gift-first, structured to be fundable.** Not as charity-idealism — as strategy. The gift frame *is* the brand (a paid app charging for "permission to speak French in New Brunswick" is psycho-logically incoherent), and the funding landscape happens to pay for exactly this shape of thing:

- **Canadian Heritage — "Appreciation and Rapprochement"** (Promotion of Linguistic Duality): funds innovative projects creating ties between English- and French-speaking Canadians, with stated priority for second-language use and dialogue between the communities. This is Bilingua's description written by a federal department.
- **Canadian Heritage — Second-Language Learning (Non-Governmental Sector):** funds "innovative methods and tools" for second-language learning; grants to $300K/yr. Prefers multi-province impact — which the "replicable playbook" framing addresses.
- **NB Bilingualism Support Program (NBBSP):** the provincial flagship for bringing the two linguistic communities closer; year-round intake.
- **Context:** the federal Action Plan for Official Languages 2023–2028 is $4.1B with a named pillar for French-second-language learning; Ottawa granted a small NB parents' FSL organization $440K+ for two years of programming (April 2026) — proof the cheque size and geography are realistic; Mauril's $16M proves Ottawa funds free bilingualism *apps* specifically, via institutions.

**The catch, and the sequencing it forces:** every one of these programs excludes individuals — a registered nonprofit must hold the grant. Three routes, in ascending order of commitment: fiscal sponsorship under an existing org; program adoption by an eligible host (CPF NB and the NB Public Library Service are the realistic candidates now that Dialogue NB is gone); or incorporating a lean NB nonprofit. Pragmatic sequence: run the first events under a host or sponsor, incorporate once funding recurs. And everywhere, in every application, the positioning gift Dialogue NB's death left behind: *the organization that did anglophone–francophone rapprochement no longer exists; this is what replaces it, rebuilt for 2026.*

**Commercial options — real, later, and deliberately deferred:** (1) employer cohort licensing, 10KC-style, to the bilingual-services industry and GNB departments that must staff 40%-bilingual-requirement positions — this is the plausible path to self-sustainability off grants; (2) the playbook itself — Ottawa/Gatineau, northern Ontario, Manitoba's franco communities, then any bilingual border region (Catalonia, South Tyrol, Wales) that wants the kit: format, coasters, software, grant templates; (3) café partnerships as revenue-neutral distribution, never as a toll. Name these in grant applications as the sustainability plan; build none of them first.

## The smallest real test

No code. Ninety days. Designed so that every possible outcome — including failure — is cheap and informative:

1. **Pick one city** (Fredericton: capital, two universities, government workforce marinated in bilingual-requirement anxiety, and the city where bilingualism actually *grew* 2001–2021).
2. **One café, one library.** Recruit a partner café for a monthly **Bilingua Night**; ask the public library to co-host (they already run conversation programming and are free).
3. **Print the alchemy.** Fifty coasters, twenty pins, table cards with the moitié-moitié rules and a conversation menu. The total physical product costs less than a month of Replit.
4. **Run three Nights, Franglish format.** Seven minutes per language, switch tables, thirty people cap. Recruit the French side first (the hard side): personally invite ten francophones as founding **Hôtes** — named, capped, honoured.
5. **Measure one thing:** how many pairs schedule a *second* conversation without being prompted. That number is the entire viability question. (The app's future job is exactly that handoff — so test the handoff with a paper signup sheet before writing a line of code.)
6. **In parallel, send two emails:** NBBSP (year-round intake) and CPF NB (fiscal host conversation). A grant application with three photographed events and a second-conversation rate in it is a different species from a grant application with a concept in it.

If the Nights fill and the second conversations happen, the app has earned its rebuild — as the coordination layer for something demonstrably alive. If they don't, you've spent three months and a few hundred dollars learning it, instead of another year of evenings.

## Reasons not to do this

Written to be actually dissuasive, because the decision deserves a real counterargument:

- **You are one person, and this is an operations job before it's a software job.** The failure mode isn't bad code; it's the seventh consecutive month of hauling coasters to a café on a Tuesday night. The timebanking graveyard is full of two-year-old projects that died with their coordinator's energy. If the honest self-assessment is "I want to build the app, not host the nights," recruit a co-founder who's the host type *before* starting, or don't start.
- **Grant funding is slow, bureaucratic, and reporting-heavy.** The money that fits this project arrives on government time, requires a nonprofit vehicle, and turns some fraction of the joy into paperwork.
- **The upside is civic, not financial.** Plan for the reward to be "New Brunswick got measurably better at talking to itself, and I did that" — with the employer-licensing path as a possible, not probable, living.
- **Meetup liability is real** even with good safety architecture; a host org or insurer conversation belongs in the first ninety days, not after an incident.
- **The 2021 census repeats in 2026–27.** If the numbers move for other reasons, the narrative urgency could soften. (Unlikely — twenty years of flatness — but noted.)

None of these are fatal. All of them are true.

## The last word

The 2025 version of Bilingua was a good app pointed at the wrong layer of the problem. It assumed the missing piece was *coordination* — help people find each other, and conversation follows. The research says otherwise: people don't fail to talk because they can't find a partner; they fail because the conversation itself costs too much face. The scarce resource was never partners. It was **permission**.

So the brilliant new angle, if it's brilliant, is this: **Bilingua is not a language-learning app. It is a permission structure with a coffee ritual, an L-plate you can put on a table, and — eventually, once it's earned — an app.** The software serves the ritual, never the reverse.

You said maybe this is a gift to the world at first. The research says the world — or at least the only officially bilingual province in Canada, sitting on the largest reservoir of silent French in the country, with a hole where its bridge-building organization used to be and two grant programs waiting for someone to claim the mandate — is unusually ready to receive it.

*Douze ans de français. Une vraie conversation.* Worth dusting off.
